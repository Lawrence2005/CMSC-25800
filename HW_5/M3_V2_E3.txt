The Adversary in the Machine
Adversarial machine learning was never a niche concern — it has, in fact, always been the central unresolved problem of artificial intelligence deployment.

There is a persistent and damaging myth in the history of machine learning: that adversarial attacks arrived as a surprise. The conventional story positions the field's adversarial turn as a moment of rupture — a 2013 paper, a perturbed image of a panda, and, with uncomfortable suddenness, the realization that deep neural networks could be fooled by noise invisible to the human eye. This narrative is wrong, and the consequences of holding it are serious. Adversarial vulnerability was not discovered in 2013; having been structurally present since the first learned classifiers were deployed against real-world adversaries, it was merely acknowledged then. The history of adversarial machine learning is not a history of surprises. It is, rather, a history of willful unpreparedness.

Origins: Spam and the First Adversaries

Long before the term "adversarial examples" entered the lexicon, practitioners in applied machine learning were grappling with an obvious truth: when a model is used to make consequential decisions, the subjects of those decisions have incentives to manipulate them. The spam filtering wars of the early 2000s are the true origin of adversarial machine learning as a practical discipline. As Bayesian classifiers became standard tools for email filtering, spammers responded almost immediately — embedding words like "mortgage" and "Viagra" inside images, scattering random benign text to dilute signal, and exploiting whitelist rules by forging trusted sender identities. This was adversarial machine learning, conducted at industrial scale, years before the academic community formalized the concept.

The 2004 CEAS workshop on machine learning and text categorization gave these problems a formal home, and researchers like Dalvi and colleagues published early theoretical treatments of adversarial classification. Their framing — anticipate the adversary's best response and design the classifier accordingly — was both rigorous and far-sighted. Yet the broader ML community did not absorb the lesson. The emerging field of deep learning, in particular, proceeded largely as though adversarial robustness were someone else's problem.

"The adversary is not an edge case. In every deployment context where stakes are real, the adversary is the median user."
The Szegedy Moment and Its Misreading

In 2013, Christian Szegedy and collaborators at Google published the paper that most accounts treat as the founding document of the field: "Intriguing Properties of Neural Networks." The finding was arresting — tiny, almost imperceptible perturbations to images could cause state-of-the-art neural networks to misclassify with high confidence. A school bus becomes an ostrich. A stop sign becomes, under adversarial manipulation, a speed limit sign. The perturbations exploited the geometry of high-dimensional learned representations in ways that seemed almost magical, and the paper's impact was both immediate and lasting.

But the misreading of this moment has been consequential. The "intriguing properties" framing — itself borrowed from the paper's title — encouraged researchers to treat adversarial examples as counterintuitive curiosities rather than as predictable consequences of how these systems were built and optimized. The follow-on work by Ian Goodfellow and others, which introduced the Fast Gradient Sign Method for efficiently generating adversarial examples, was framed as an empirical finding about neural networks specifically. In fact, the deeper point applies to any learned model: models trained to minimize empirical risk on a training distribution will generalize poorly under distribution shift caused by an intelligent adversary. The excitement around deep neural networks' specific vulnerabilities, though understandable, obscured the generality of the problem.

The Arms Race That Wasn't Won

What followed the 2013 moment was characterized by a productive but ultimately frustrating arms race dynamic. Researchers proposed defenses; other researchers, often quickly, broke them. Adversarial training — augmenting the training set with adversarial examples — emerged as the most robust approach, but it came at significant cost to performance on clean inputs and with no guarantee of robustness against novel attack types. Certified defenses, which aimed to provide mathematical guarantees of robustness within bounded perturbation radii, represented genuine progress but scaled poorly to large models and realistic threat models.

The persistence of this dynamic reflects something important: adversarial robustness is in fundamental tension with generalization as it is currently understood. A model that perfectly interpolates a training distribution will, almost by definition, extrapolate poorly when the test distribution is shaped by an adversary aware of the model's weaknesses. The theoretical work of Bubeck, Price, Razenshteyn and others has shown that robust classifiers likely require significantly more parameters and data than their non-robust counterparts. Robustness, in other words, is not a property that can be bolted on after the fact. From the beginning, it had to be designed in — and the field spent roughly a decade pretending otherwise.

Physical-World Attacks and the Stakes Increase

If the early adversarial ML literature could still be treated as a theoretical curiosity, work by Eykholt and colleagues in 2018 foreclosed that escape route. Their demonstration that carefully placed stickers on stop signs could cause autonomous vehicle vision systems to misclassify them under real-world conditions brought the stakes into sharp relief. This was not a perturbation computed in gradient space and applied to a test image; rather, it was a physical object that reliably fooled classifiers in the field across lighting conditions, angles, and distances.

The implications for the deployment of machine learning in safety-critical systems — autonomous vehicles, medical imaging, facial recognition for access control — were severe and, despite that severity, were largely not acted upon with appropriate urgency. The field's response was, characteristically, to generate more papers documenting the problem. The connection between adversarial vulnerability in the laboratory and adversarial vulnerability in deployed systems has, in most industrial contexts, still not been adequately institutionalized.

Language Models and the Expanding Attack Surface

The ascent of large language models from 2020 onward introduced adversarial dynamics at a different layer of abstraction. Prompt injection attacks — in which malicious instructions embedded in inputs hijack model behavior — are, structurally speaking, adversarial examples in the semantic domain. They exploit the same fundamental property: models trained to be helpful and instruction-following in the standard case will remain helpfully instruction-following when the instructions themselves are adversarial. The boundary between "data" and "instruction" that is obvious to a human reader is not, for transformer architectures, a distinction they reliably enforce.

The history here has a bitter irony. The adversarial ML community had, for a decade, been arguing that the distinction between clean and adversarial inputs was not one that current architectures could reliably be trusted to maintain. Prompt injection demonstrated this in a context — text, natural language, business applications — that was impossible to dismiss as a laboratory artifact. Yet the deployment of large language models in agentic contexts, where models are granted access to tools and the ability to take real-world actions, proceeded rapidly and, in many cases, with insufficient attention to this well-documented failure mode.

"Prompt injection is not a novel attack. It is adversarial machine learning, conducted in natural language, against systems whose designers forgot the lessons of the spam wars."
The Institutional Failure

What the history of adversarial machine learning ultimately documents is not a technical failure but an institutional one. The technical community produced, repeatedly and with increasing precision, analyses of exactly how and why these systems were vulnerable. The deployment community — including large technology firms, government agencies, and the startups that have industrialized machine learning applications — consistently chose to treat adversarial robustness as a secondary concern to be addressed only after the primary goals of accuracy and capability were achieved. This ordering has it exactly backward.

In every domain where machine learning systems interact with adversaries — and every year that list grows longer — robustness is not a nice-to-have. It is the threshold property that determines whether a system is safe to deploy at all. A facial recognition system that is 99% accurate on a clean test set and reliably fooled by a pair of printed glasses is not, in any meaningful sense, a 99% accurate system. It is a broken system with a false accuracy estimate. The field's metrics have, from the beginning, been designed to measure performance against the best case — a test distribution drawn from the same population as the training set, with no intelligent actor attempting to manipulate outcomes. This is almost never the real case.

Toward a More Honest History

The honest account of adversarial machine learning's history is one of a community that knew, or at minimum should have known, the risks of deploying learned models against intelligent adversaries — and that repeatedly chose capability and performance over robustness. The spam filterers of the early 2000s understood this. The academic adversarial ML community, from 2004 onward, understood this as well. The lesson was not learned, or rather not institutionalized, because the incentives of the field — benchmark performance, publication novelty, commercial deployment timelines — all pointed elsewhere.

This history matters not as a counsel of despair but as a diagnosis. The adversarial vulnerability of machine learning systems is not a mysterious property of deep learning specifically, nor is it a problem that will simply be solved by the next generation of architectures. It is a consequence of how we train, evaluate, and deploy these systems — and, until those practices change, it will persist. The adversary has always been in the machine. The question is whether, finally, we will take that seriously.
